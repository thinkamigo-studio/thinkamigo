## The Creative Framework

*I view the landscape, shape the stories, build the platform and share with others.*

It's been this way since 1968, maybe before. It applies equally to a seven-year-old drawing comics, a touring exhibition reaching 70,000 visitors, a government service reaching millions, or a 90,000 word manuscript nearing completion.

As a teaching framework — the advice to students, the keynote closer:

**View the landscape**
**Shape the stories**
**Build the platform**
**Share with others**

Four steps. Each one in service of the next. Viewing the landscape is the curiosity impulse — observation, research, standing back before acting. Stories are what that impulse becomes when it finds form. The platform is how the story gets shaped, built and carried. Sharing with others is pure delivery — the moment the story engages the audience.

Platform Development is how the stories get carried. It is not the overarching frame. The Creative Framework is the overarching frame.

## The Making Impulse

He drew comics from the age of seven. At thirteen he was making cine films. At fifteen — three years ahead of the normal age — he passed his A Level Art exam. Two years later, in 1978, he skipped an art history lesson and got on a train to London carrying paintings — a portrait submitted to the Mall Galleries, an abstract piece for Young Contemporaries. The portrait was selected. Young Contemporaries said no. The bravado was good either way. Nobody sent him. The history of art could wait. The making of it couldn't.

The National Union of Mineworkers commissioned a painting for their branch HQ — it appeared in A.R. Griffin's *The Nottinghamshire Coalfield, 1881–1981: A Century of Progress* (1981), alongside an honorary membership of the NUM. A CISWO scholarship followed — the Coal Industry Social Welfare Organisation investing in one of its own.

The creative impulse has never stopped. It has driven everything since: the satirical comics, the cine films, the band someone called Beatleish and beautiful, the broadcast appearances, the heritage platforms, the government design work, the 90,000 word manuscript nearing completion.

It is the compulsive, showy, attention-seeking traits of a practising artist. The artistic sensibility, carefully restrained in the corporate environment, contrasting with the freedom afforded by arts and humanities funding.

This is the origin of everything else in this brief.

**The USP — Platform Thinking**

Platform thinking is the defining capability that runs through every project across four decades. Not web design, not digital marketing, not UX, not content strategy — though all of those are components of it. Platform thinking is the ability to conceive content, architecture and audience as a single unified system, before any of the parts exist. It includes the pipelines that build and deliver the services — not just how the platform is used, but how it is constructed.

The through line is consistent across every context: who is this for, how do they reach it, what happens when they get there, what gets in the way, and what are the pipelines that make it possible? The scale changes. The institutional context changes. The underlying question never does.

At HM Revenue and Customs, platform thinking is applied to one of the most complex government platforms in existence — understanding the pipelines that build those services from the inside and how real users navigate services that 40 million people depend on.

**Early Career — The Sequence**

**Nottinghamshire Fire and Rescue (1984–85)** — the question facing a fine art graduate leaving art school: what job can you do? Paul could handle a camera and had been developing photographs since acquiring a darkroom enlarger from his woodwork teacher in 1975. Nottinghamshire Fire and Rescue, Audio Visual Department provided the answer.

A job share with videographer Dave Halls — a drummer who would later collaborate on post-Smart Cookies recording sessions alongside guitarist Chris Richards. Working for Cyril Brocklehurst, a crotchety staff photographer of limited ability. The Chief Fire Officer quietly ensured Dave and Paul handled anything that mattered.

Three streams of work ran simultaneously:

*Fire Prevention* — training materials and public communications. The making impulse finding a public information purpose.

*Forensic Investigation* — documenting fire scenes and road traffic accidents for analysis and legal evidence. The trained eye for what an image reveals that the scene conceals. Precision, objectivity, the photograph as record rather than expression.

*Public Relations* — two significant public engagements, both documented by Dave and Paul.

The official opening of Nottinghamshire Fire and Rescue HQ at Bestwood Lodge — a Victorian Gothic mansion — with HRH Prince Charles and Princess Diana in attendance. Dave and Paul given the real responsibility, Paul equipped with a JVC video camera and portable Sony U-Matic recorder. A Certificate of Merit awarded by the Chief Fire Officer for the quality of the coverage.

The National Conference on Sports Ground Safety at the City Ground, Nottingham — convened in the aftermath of the Bradford City Stadium Fire in which 56 people died and 265 were injured, attended by leading representatives from football, cricket, horse-racing and emergency services from across the UK. The video footage from Bradford shown at the conference was horrendous — bodies on fire. The reason everyone was in the room made viscerally present. Brian Clough was present — one of the most iconic, charismatic and outspoken figures in English football, a man whose force of personality was as legendary as his two European Cup wins with Nottingham Forest. Paul photographed Clough as he met with civic dignitaries, posing him with Chief Fire Officer Peter Wilson and Assistant CFO Jerry Lane. Ever professional, Clough maintained his pose through each recharge of the Metz flashgun, deploying one of his famous modes of address: *'Come on Young Man, hurry up, or I'll give you another bloody earring.'*

The subsequent BBC interview was pure Clough — the master of the soundbite, responding to the government threat to close football grounds, knowing exactly what the cameras would take and the national news would use. In his own words, in his own voice: *'Let me tell you this. If Margaret Thatcher wants to close anything down, then let her close 10 Downing Street down.'* Direct, unedited, perfect first time.

The encounter was later written up for Dawn of the Unread, where Paul is credited as co-producer. In the comic, Clough appears as **Byron Clough** — a composite of two of Nottinghamshire's most outspoken and rebellious figures: Lord Byron and Brian Clough. Both men were larger than life, contemptuous of received opinion, impossible to ignore, and entirely Nottinghamshire's own.

Two major multi-agency emergency simulation exercises documented in full:

*Exercise One - Exercise Hessian* — a simulated fire and explosion at a Boots chemical site, involving Boots' own fire service alongside the county emergency services. Rivalry, camaraderie and very dark humour between professional firefighters and an industrial fire brigade.

*Exercise Two — The Real Emergency* — a full 48-hour simulation designed to exhaust the services as they dealt with a serious environmental incident. Staff grilled by reporters, footage edited in real time, a studio set up for interviews. The theatre so realistic that in the early hours, exhausted, an ambulance driver broke down in front of the camera. The boundary between simulation and genuine distress dissolving under fatigue.

**Ramba Zamba** — the successor to Smart Cookies, developed in the margins of working lives with limited rehearsal time. Core group: Paul Fillingham (voice), Dave Halls (drums), Chris Richards (guitar), Adam Fisher (keyboards). Live rock with MIDI synthesisers, samplers and a BBC Micro running the UMI2B MIDI sequencer — Smart Cookies had been using MIDI from 1983, one of the earliest adopters of the standard. In 1985, Ramba Zamba's Ensoniq Mirage sampler loading patches from floppy disc in real time — the terror of live electronic performance, visible and precarious, no safety net.

Bestwood Lodge provided rehearsal space. Firefighter Paul MacKenzie — also a BBC Radio Derby DJ — heard the music and gave Ramba Zamba airplay. MacKenzie then appeared in video segments produced by Dave and Paul, edited on the Sony U-Matic suite at Clarendon College, playing in the gaps between songs during live performances — branded as the **Video GrooveBox**. The dead time between patch loads turned into content, the live performance as a total media environment. Pre-edited video sequences running the full length of a set. A transmedia live performance concept in 1985, years before the idea had a name.

Live performances didn't happen until 1988-89 — the production infrastructure existed long before the band was ready to use it in public. The making impulse building the show before the show was ready to happen.

**KRCS AppleCentre Group — Creative Services Manager** — returned to KRCS at the invitation of MD Ken Woods, who asked what had been learned during the intervening period and shared plans for rapid expansion. The expanded **KRCS Group PLC** became a value added reseller for Agfa, Linotype and Monotype hardware — kitting out design studios and publishing houses was serious money, expensive kit, big sales. The dedicated sales team closed the deals. Paul's role was different — the initial meeting, the soft sell, the hand-holding once the client was equipped. Apple Research Fellow, Guy Kawasaki coined the term **Evangelist**, to describe this kind of role: Not selling the product — making people believe in what it could do for them.

After company expansion through venture capital acquisition into London and the Home Counties, adopted a strategic role — brand guardian for the group, localising Apple collateral from the US, handling all communications. In Mike Conwill's words, before he headhunted Paul to set up their digital studio: *a one man advertising agency*. Designed 3D models for exhibition stands. Had access to pre-release software and hardware — digital cameras, printers, professional imagesetters. Worked with industry partners Agfa and Linotype at the exact hinge point where the industry was transitioning from traditional to digital pre-press.

The disruption was real and brutal. Businesses that didn't make the transition didn't survive. Early 90's using AppleLink email and BT Kilostream connections to transport large pre-press files for remote printing — networked digital production before the public internet existed. Clients included newspaper groups, publishers, advertising agencies and corporates. They either bought the kit and got on board, or their business went to the wall. Paul was there to hold their hand.

The evangelist role wasn't just enthusiasm — it was conviction held against active derision. Serious journalists in design magazines were writing that email would never catch on, while Paul was using it to correspond with the people who were building the future. You had to be an evangelist to combat that kind of resistance. The adoption of each new technology was never just early adoption — it was a considered position maintained against the grain of received wisdom.

That hand-holding — the person who understands both the technology and the human fear of it — runs as a thread through everything that followed. James Walker and David Amos. The GDS accessibility work. The heritage platform workshops. Always the person who makes the transition possible by being present and trustworthy.

*Raleigh Interactive (1995)* — pitched the concept of CDi-based interactive point of sale kiosks to Raleigh, an existing CHC client. The architecture was mapped on Mike Conwill's kitchen table in working sessions that followed the Broadway Cinema first meeting. Quality video playback was beyond the capabilities of standard PCs and the client required over one hundred units. Philips CDi format players enhanced with MPEG video cards provided the solution. Working at 720x576px resolution allowed blending of product photography, text and video, effectively repurposing the Raleigh print catalogue and TV advertising for an interactive point of sale unit robust enough for cycle shop environments. CDi disc mastering performed at Philips before read-write CD-ROMs became commonplace, duplicated in Germany, distributed to over one hundred dealerships with freestanding rental units. Featured in an IMRG white paper. Keynote presentation in London to showcase the technology.

*Raleigh Advertising — Cerne Abbas Giant* — a Photoshopped chalk Raleigh bicycle placed adjacent to the well-endowed Neolithic figure. As AdLine reported: *'Those cheeky chaps at Cross Hill Conwill in Nottingham are claiming to be the first to feature a tumescent male organ in a national advertising campaign.'* Published as a double page spread in the male style press. Mike wrote the copy, Paul did the visuals. That Raleigh's Marketing Director was a woman — Yvonne Rix — speaks to how brave the client was and how much mutual trust existed. *Raleigh Advertising — Pioneer London Underground Poster* — a photomontage campaign for the women's Pioneer bicycle, placed on London Underground. The treatment: a Gainsborough — Mrs Richard Brinsley Sheridan, one of the most celebrated portraits in the National Gallery, a woman seated in a woodland setting — with a Raleigh Pioneer faithfully composited into the scene. Oil paint craquelure carefully matched and applied so the bicycle appeared to have been painted by Gainsborough himself, the join invisible. An enormous Photoshop file that pushed the studio Mac to its limits. Conceptually precise, technically invisible — a masterpiece of photomontage.

*Raleigh Advertising — Activator Mountain Bike TV Campaign* — a completely separate campaign. A race between a team cyclist and a snowboarder on a snow-covered mountain, edited to a rap soundtrack. Mike directed the shoot, Paul created the videographics. The ad was strong but CHC's media buying strategy was the real genius — multiple five-second clips scheduled between other TV advertisements, just enough for two syllables to register, announcing a single product feature. Each ad break followed this subliminal synapse sequence and ended with a brand logo, a silhouette and the voiceover: *Designed in America. Built in Nottingham England.* The crafty ads created an outrage, were pulled after a few weeks and new rules put in place so it could not be repeated.

Twelve years at Headland. A deliberately diverse portfolio. A methodology — user-centred, information architecture-led, commercially grounded — sharpened to the point where the next chapter was the logical destination.

**Driving Standards Agency — Young Drivers (2008)** — while still at Headland, a connection through Lavanya Sivakumaran led to a freelance commission for the DSA. A multi-platform social media campaign across Facebook, Bebo and MySpace driving traffic to a dedicated microsite — the first social media campaign in the project history, predating most agencies' social media practice by several years. The microsite featured cartoon characters illustrated by Ruth Disney, a trusted collaborator from the CHC years. Each character had their own managed social media profile — alternate reality game thinking applied to a government road safety brief, four years before GDS existed. The campaign also featured vox pop video with real young drivers, with post production and web embedding handled by Paul. The response was exceptional. The campaign directly prefigures the Houdini Window approach — fictional characters with independent online presences, the fiction distributed across multiple platforms simultaneously, the audience drawn in rather than pushed at.

He is currently working to a deliberate five-year horizon of focused creative and commercial activity. This is not a winding-down. It is a purposeful concentration of energy — transitioning from corporate work to more arts and humanities projects, completing and releasing Houdini Window, and establishing the writing practice as the primary creative activity. The door remains open for the right project, right package.

## The Three Core Activities

### 1. Gov and Enterprise UR/UX

Senior UX researcher and designer working within UK Government Digital Service (GDS) framework. Currently contracting inside IR35, rolling six-month engagements.

UK Government engagements include HM Revenue and Customs, Driving and Vehicle Standards Agency, Department for Education, Nuclear Decommissioning Authority, Coal Remediation Authority, the National Resilience Extranet, along with City, District, Parish councils and emergency services. Work is Agile, multidisciplinary, accessibility-first. The Six Thinking Hats methodology — adapted from de Bono for UX by Paul Fillingham — is used actively and taught to others.

Research methods, analysis and reporting are essentially storytelling. The Creative Framework operates here as in every other context — viewing the landscape of user insight, shaping it into a narrative the organisation can act on, building the research platform, and sharing with others who need to hear it. Security, NDA and contractual constraints prevent public articulation of specific cases.

### 2. Digital Arts Platforms

Platforms that carry creative work and cultural narratives. The roots of this activity go back further than is commonly acknowledged.

**Rufford Country Park and National Ceramics Centre (c.1999)** — at Headland Multimedia, Lottery funded interpretation work including VR walkthroughs, touchscreen interpretation units and CD-ROM. Digital arts platform work in public heritage contexts over a decade before the Sillitoe Trail.

**Empedia (2010–11)** — at Cuttlefish Multimedia with Sean Clark. A Renaissance East Midlands funded portal for multiple cultural trails across the East Midlands region, with some experimental trails beyond the region. Where the methodology of building a platform was tested and refined — media ingest, mobile and location-based applications, physical QR code signage, Google Maps integration.

**The Sillitoe Trail (2012)** — began as a prototype developed by Paul Fillingham, James Walker and David Sillitoe (Alan's son), following introductions made at the Nottingham Writers Studio. With the blessing of the Alan Sillitoe Memorial Committee, and support from John Cairns at Arts Council England and Susannah Simons at the BBC, the Sillitoe Trail was selected as one of 50 projects for The Space — a unique collaboration between the BBC and Arts Council England to develop an experimental on-demand digital arts platform. The only literary project from outside London.

The project was developed during Paul's recovery from a minor stroke. One month in — double vision, difficulty walking, spaced out — his wife drove them to London for the pitch. Paul wore dark glasses to hide an eye patch. They got the project. Triumph over adversity. Eleven months of recovery with the Sillitoe Trail keeping him sane.

Over eighteen months the project became an incubator for digital storytelling — the BBC developing digital ingest pipelines for traditional programme-making, Arts Council England engaging audiences in theatres, galleries and live events, with creative contributions from over 70 local artists, writers, poets, filmmakers and musicians. The Space was launched by BBC Director General Mark Thompson, Don Letts and Will Self at the Royal Festival Hall, and subsequently championed by Alan Yentob, ACE chair Sir Peter Bazalgette and the Google Cultural Institute. Featured on BBC Inside Out and BBC Radio 4's Saturday Live with John McCarthy.

**Dawn of the Unread** — built in genuine creative partnership with James Walker, originating organically. Conceived whilst giving James a lift home from town.

paulfillingham.com and the Substack are the current platforms in this activity. Seaside Sci-fi — a coastal community arts network anchored at the Winter Gardens, Morecambe — is in development.

### The Accessibility Thread

Literacy, Heritage and GOV work converge at a single point: accessibility. This is not a design principle borrowed from GDS — it is the intellectual core of everything Thinkamigo does, arrived at independently from three directions simultaneously.

**Literacy** — accessible content is clear, direct, unambiguous. The same principles that make a document readable to someone with low literacy make it better for everyone. The adult literacy Community Service Announcement scripted for ITV Central in the Clarendon College years was the earliest expression of this — public communication as a design problem with real human stakes.

**Heritage** — digital exclusion means communities cannot access their own history. The metadata work, the eyewitness capture, the Coal Authority archive — all of it is meaningless if the people it is about cannot reach it. Mine2Minds Education was established precisely to address this. It is estimated that over 9 million people in the UK lack the necessary skills to use the internet without assistance. 5.4 million lack even the basic ability to switch on a digital device. (Source: Lloyds Bank UK Consumer Digital Index, 2020.) Former mining communities within the East Midlands — Mansfield ranked 299th out of 324 towns and cities in national surveys of health, crime, economic and educational achievement (Source: ONS data, September 2019, updated May 2020) — suffer from particularly low levels of digital inclusion. The heritage work is not separable from the digital inclusion work. They are the same argument made two different ways.

**GOV** — the GDS design principles were built specifically around the reality that government services must work for everyone, including those 9 million. That is where the research rigour comes from: the user testing, the plain language standards, the accessibility compliance, the insistence on designing for the most excluded user rather than the median one. Paul encountered Jeremy Paxman at Bilsthorpe Mining Heritage Museum alongside Dr David Amos and Professor Natalie Braber whilst Paxman was conducting primary research for *Black Gold: The History of How Coal Made Britain* (2021) — a meeting that speaks to the credibility the Mine2Minds work had built with serious researchers and broadcasters.

Three different contexts. One underlying question: who gets left out, and why?

The Thinkamigo framework itself is an expression of this principle. The restraint, the typographic clarity, the absence of decorative noise, the open source HTML/CSS/JS with no framework dependencies — a well-designed site that works on any device and loads cleanly is itself a small act in the right direction. The GDS interaction patterns — the breadcrumb underline that thickens on hover, the plain language, the logical hierarchy — are not borrowed aesthetics. They are borrowed ethics.

### Transatlantic Projects

Two significant transatlantic heritage and cultural projects — both representing the methodology at its most ambitious geographic scale, both currently stalled by the US political climate.

**United by Stories** — a collaborative transatlantic digital heritage platform connecting the UK East Midlands and the United States through geo-referenced stories. Developed with Roger Williams University in Rhode Island. Beta Version 1.0 launched October 2024. Funding opportunities arranged with the university were derailed within months by the change in US political climate. A really disappointing dead end for work that was ready to go.

**TransAtlantic Rebel Counties** — a comparative study of coal mining cultural heritage across the Atlantic, connecting the rebel counties of Nottinghamshire and Durham with Illinois. Collaboration between Mine2Minds, University of Illinois, Nottingham Trent University and the University of Cambridge. Active but under threat for the same reasons.

Both platforms remain live. Both relationships remain intact. The political situation is monitored.

Platforms that preserve and interpret community memory — and are catalysts for fresh, new interpretation. The body of work here — Mine2Minds, MyTrail, the coalfield projects with Dr David Amos — represents more than a decade of sustained commitment to documenting Nottinghamshire mining heritage.

The CISWO scholarship that funded Paul's art education is the origin of this work. The mining community invested in one of its own. The heritage practice is the payback — honoured now in full across multiple projects, publications, films, platforms and exhibitions reaching over 70,000 visitors.

This activity is reaching a natural point of consolidation. The focus is broadening to Morecambe, the North West, and new MyTrail territory.

**MyTrail** — a purpose-built digital heritage interpretation platform currently hosting six trails across five sites. Platform-independent navigation, audio, video, archive maps. Migration from WordPress to open source HTML/CSS/JS planned.

## The Writing Practice

This is the destination. The place the entire career has been building toward.

Houdini Window is a reclamation — the avant-garde position reasserted after decades of deferred creative energy. It is not a return to something. It is a return to form. The corporate work was the interruption. The work continues.

Paul Fillingham is a 1960s optimist. Formed by the Space Age, by the genuine belief that technology could take you somewhere extraordinary, he has greeted every new tool — Photoshop, HTML, GAN, AI — with the same appetite. Not because it's new but because it might be the thing that gets you there. What he is doing now is avant-garde. Like his paintings from art school days.

### Origin

The conceptual journey began around 2010 when Marcus Clarke — best friend, professional puppeteer, Jim Henson Creature Shop veteran — introduced Paul to the Nottingham Writers Studio. Together they developed *The Puppetmaster* — an irreverent comedy script featuring an elderly puppeteer and a young technologist. Photo-collage moodboards in Photoshop, a TV screenplay in Word, Marcus contributing comedic dialogue and characters. The project was shelved — literally in a garden shed — when a Canadian TV comedy, *Puppets That Kill*, appeared with a similar theme.

The garden shed screenplay nearly didn't survive. When it resurfaced in 2024 the spiral-bound document was damp and damaged, with no electronic copy readily available.

During Covid lockdown, Paul and Adrian Reynolds began collaborating on fictional blog posts drawing on the unsettling nature of the pandemic. When the Puppetmaster screenplay resurfaced, they were also developing ideas for a wartime story set in Sherwood Forest. The two threads merged. Without the comedic elements, the story took on a darker, more contemporary tone — quantum physics, pervasive technology, AI, seemingly random events with devastating consequences.

### The Novel

**Houdini Window** — approximately 90,000 words, nearing completion. When David Lovelace's teleport experiment goes wrong, he witnesses an attack on his grandfather — legendary entertainer Laurie Lovelace — in the fictional Lincolnshire seaside town of Binley Bay. The incident unravels a legacy of family secrets connecting Cold War America, swinging sixties London, paranormal espionage and a collection of mystical artefacts.

It is Paul's project. Adrian Reynolds acts as an experienced editorial eye — the novel is not co-authored. Non-linear structure, dual timelines, surface normality concealing hidden systems. The Kingsley-Lovelace Archive as the novel's spine — objects as memory systems at their most explicit.

The intelligence and hidden systems thread running through the novel is not invented. Paul's background includes work as an investigation photographer for Nottinghamshire Fire and Rescue, and design and research projects with the Central Office for Information, the National Resilience Extranet, the Nuclear Decommissioning Authority and the Royal Air Force. The architecture of sensitive, classified data informs the clandestine undercurrents of the story.

The Houdini Window website will not be a standard author site. The website itself is integral to the lore of the story — the site as narrative device, the platform as part of the fiction. The Thinkamigo framework was always the rehearsal for this. The Blair Witch Project (1999) demonstrated the power of a website that presents itself as evidence rather than promotion. Houdini Window takes a different approach — but the same principle applies. The framework's restraint, credibility and editorial sensibility are the right foundation for a site that needs to feel like a genuine cold war archive that happens to be online.

It will not be the only novel.

### The AI Production Pipeline

Prior to ChatGPT, Paul had written about Nvidia's Generative Adversarial Networks — thispersondoesist.com in 2019, realistic portraits generated from Flickr-scraped training data. The technology has been developing alongside the novel ever since.

The production pipeline (as of October 2025):

**Stills** — character images generated via GPT prompts in Perplexity and Gemini. The Mike Leigh prompt discovery — introducing the reference produced more lifelike British characters and settings. Images composited in Adobe Photoshop using greenscreen cutouts.

**Video** — composited stills processed in LTX Studio for short video sequences. Lip-synced with audio using KlingAI.

**Audio** — TTS voices from Speechify and ElevenLabs reading dialogue directly from the manuscript. Musical themes generated in Suno AI, providing multitracked stems mixed in Logic Pro. Assembled in Adobe Audition for audiobook and podcast production. TTS used for proofreading during evening walks — edits made on the fly via mobile and Google Docs.

**Long-form video** — assembled in Adobe Premiere, mixed with library footage, with effects added for realism.

Audio versions of Substack pieces narrated by Debbie Erwin. Episodic release planned across novel, audiobook, podcast and video.

## Formation

**The 108 Club — The Founding Document**

Before Leeds Polytechnic, before Clarendon College, before the Apple Mac, before the professional career — there was the 108 Club. Founded by Paul Fillingham and his childhood friend Steven Clay on Central Avenue, Blidworth, in the early 1970s. Every element of the professional practice is present in embryo.

The briefcase with the maps — information architecture before the term existed, planning activities weeks in advance with military precision. The newsletter reproduced on a secondhand photocopier — publishing, distribution, audience, a subscription model funding the operation. The Standard 8mm cine camera — moving image production, the making impulse finding its medium at the earliest possible age. The professional-looking title sequence on every film — branding, the craft standard set from the beginning. The garage screenings, kids from all over the village sitting spellbound — platform and audience in one room, step four of the Creative Framework at age ten.

Roy gave the discipline — the work ethic, the moral standards, the covering for his mates, the seeing that everyone was OK. Valerie gave the welcome — the revolving door at Appleton Road, the room at the table for waifs and strays, the Auntie Val who fed and housed the village. The 108 Club combined both. So has every project since.

Steve Clay co-founded that with Paul at age ten or eleven. The last conversation they had alive was on the former site of the Blidworth Miners' Welfare, talking about the urgency of capturing living memory before it's gone. He understood what the work was for. He always had.

The kitchen table as the site of serious creative work — Steve's kitchen table on Central Avenue, the Broadway Cinema boardroom table with Mike Conwill, the mindmap and the foamex prototype. The best work happened informally, between people who trusted each other, working from instinct and shared intent.

**Blidworth, Nottinghamshire** — a former mining village in Sherwood Forest. Birthplace of the Luddite movement. Newstead Abbey, Byron's estate, two miles away. This landscape is the foundation, not as nostalgia but as a worldview rooted in community, hidden histories and the human cost of industrial change.

**London, 1967** — a fortnight that changed everything. Taken to the capital at six by his father Roy and his father's friend Mal — a miner convalescing from an underground fire — Paul fell in love with the city. The Planetarium, the Zoo, the Science Museum, the Natural History Museum, the Imperial War Museum, the National Portrait Gallery, Carnaby Street at its cultural peak, the London Underground. Two men whose working lives were spent underground choosing to show a six-year-old the world above ground in its fullest possible expression. When he works in London now, his mind goes back to that visit. The city has never lost the charge it acquired then.

**Roy and Valerie Fillingham** — the formation runs deeper than landscape. Roy Fillingham, face-worker at Blidworth Colliery for 35 years, one of the first men at the pit to drive a cutting machine on the coalface, later a Deputy who presided over the colliery closure in 1989. A man of absolute consistency — the work ethic, the humour, the camaraderie, the going to China (which meant the Bird in Hand). Valerie Wass, youngest of seven, fit and athletic, ran for the Sutton Harriers, could belt out Gracie Fields at the top of her voice and was pitch perfect. Known to most of the village as Auntie Val. The revolving door at Appleton Road. The midnight chip cobs. The four sons — Paul, Peter, Mark, Michael — and later Gilbert, Paul's son, whom Valerie called her fifth. Roy died in 2018 after 61 years of marriage. Valerie in 2025, having seen Roy four times in the hospital corridors — *'And if I see him again, I'm going with him'* — and she did. Roy came for her whilst the nurse and Mark were out of the room. *'I married him you know.'* Said twice, just in case anyone missed it.

The rock and roll formation is inherited. Roy's Tommy Steele and Joe Brown records. Valerie adding Kate Bush to her repertoire in 1978. The music that formed Smart Cookies, Ramba Zamba, and the entire sonic sensibility — it starts in the front room at Appleton Road.

**Nottingham's unholy trinity** — Byron, D.H. Lawrence, Alan Sillitoe. Three rebel writers whose work directly informs the practice.

**Clarendon College, Nottingham (1978–1980)** — a two year Art, Design and Art History course. Where de Bono's Six Thinking Hats were first encountered, where Richard Brautigan was introduced by an American friend from Marin County, where the formation that preceded Leeds was assembled. Eight years later he returned as a member of staff — the student who borrowed ideas becoming the technician who borrowed the Mac at weekends to teach himself the future.

**Leeds Polytechnic, Fine Art, 1980–1983** — modelled on the Bauhaus multidisciplinary approach. Tutor Jeff Nuttall, author of *Bomb Culture* and Beat poet. The formation: discipline as a creative principle, form follows function, all disciplines in service of a unified idea. From Mansfield to Nottingham to Leeds, he found his people. The sense of belonging that came from being around others who made things, thought differently, saw the world sideways.

**Musical formation** — progressive rock first (ambition, concept, technical seriousness), then Bowie (identity as performance, the outsider as protagonist), then New York art-punk: Talking Heads, the Ramones — New York not London, the art school sensibility. New wave and electronic culture alongside. Smart Cookies, the Leeds Polytechnic band: guitar and synthesisers, melody-first, no drummer. One 1984 review called their song *Loud and Lonely* Beatleish and beautiful. The music practice is complete. The Leeds Arts University show in 2025 was the right farewell.

**Critical sensibility** — Art history lessons were loved, not endured. The natural gravitation was always toward the rebels — artists with interesting lives, work that challenged convention and drove fellow students and lecturers nuts, in a good way. Carl Andre's *Equivalent VIII* — demonised by the tabloid press as a waste of public money — was championed without hesitation. Fluxus performance artists from the 1960s and 70s, with Jeff Nuttall among the participants. The Americana of British Pop — Richard Hamilton and Eduardo Paolozzi, whose collaged mythologies of consumer culture connected directly to the formation. Gilbert and George, met several times. Paolozzi, met at the Tate. The student who argued for the bricks is the same person who has spent a career arguing for overlooked communities, marginal histories and work that doesn't fit the sanctioned narrative.

**Cinema** — *If....* (Lindsay Anderson, 1968) as formative recognition, not just cinema. Kurosawa, Tarkovsky, Kubrick. 1960s British kitchen sink. *Easy Rider* and Jack Nicholson toasting D.H. Lawrence.

**Edward de Bono** — Six Thinking Hats, learned at Clarendon College before Leeds. De Bono's insight that humour is an important marker of intellect — because humour requires the mind to make unexpected lateral connections — is load bearing for the entire practice.

**Richard Brautigan** — introduced by an American friend at Clarendon College. Peculiar lightness, whimsy shot through with something darker. Runs through the writing voice.

**Spike Milligan** — *Adolf Hitler: My Part in His Downfall*, read on a Butlins monorail in Skegness. The formative experience of dark comedy as a serious literary mode. Roy's favourite too — the Goons, Milligan, Marx Brothers, Monty Python. The humour is inherited.

**Arena (BBC2, 1978)** — encountered at seventeen, the same year as the train to London. Alan Yentob's Arena wasn't just arts television — it was a poem disguised as television, a rich network of ideas, mixed-media before the digital age had begun. Recorded on a portable cassette recorder, the screen photographed with a Soviet-made Zenith-e 35mm camera. Replayed until the words were memorised. The screenshots printed onto photo paper, distressed by 625 analogue scan lines, became collage material for sketchbooks — everything deconstructed and rearranged into something new. A single programme broadcast on a winter's night could ripple through the imagination, inspiring new ways of seeing and making. Met Yentob in person at the Royal Festival Hall in 2012 at The Space launch — a forty year arc from watching Arena in Blidworth to standing in the same room.

**James Burke — Connections (BBC, 1978)** — encountered at seventeen, before critical vocabulary existed for what Burke was doing. Following the thread wherever it leads without announcing the destination in advance. Trusting the audience to keep up. The direct ancestor of the Creative Framework.

## Brand Voice

**The statement of practice:** *I view the landscape, shape the stories, build the platform and share with others.*

**The core description:** Paul Fillingham views the landscape, shapes it into stories, builds the platforms to carry them, and speaks directly to the people they are for. He has been doing this since 1968. The medium changes. The act is always the same.

**Primary title:** Digital producer. On paulfillingham.com and in the writing practice context: Writer.

**What Paul is:** A systems thinker with an artist's soul. A 1960s optimist who has embraced every new technology as a potential vehicle for the work. Rooted without being parochial. Rigorous without being academic. Technologically fluent without being technocratic. Commercially engaged without being corporate. Funny — de Bono was right about humour and intelligence.

**Tone:** Direct and considered. Warmth without sentimentality. Intellectual curiosity without jargon. The craft is visible but worn lightly. Serious about the work, not precious about it. Humour earned rather than performed.

**What to avoid:** Corporate language. Breathless enthusiasm about technology for its own sake. Nostalgia — the past is load bearing, not load limiting. Consolation dressed as strategy. "Hand-rolled" — say "open source" instead.

## Design and Technical Principles

**Aesthetic position:** Minimal. Restrained. Typography-led. Bauhaus → Swiss International Style → GDS. Whitespace as a design element. Decoration earns its place or is removed.

**The accessibility principle:** Subtle, accessible, stylish. These are not three separate goals — they are one. Clarity of purpose expressed through restraint of means. The GDS interaction patterns — the breadcrumb underline that thickens on hover, plain language, logical hierarchy — are borrowed ethics, not borrowed aesthetics. Good design doesn't announce itself.

**CSS architecture:**
- `chassis.css` — structural foundation
- `destination.css` — property-specific skin
- `gallery.css` — image components
- `theatre.css` — video player styles

This separation allows Thinkamigo, Houdini Window, Seaside Sci-fi and paulfillingham.com to share the same framework while maintaining distinct visual identities.

**Design system rules:** Poppins only. Sentence case throughout. Hex-only colour values. `--brand-blue: #0054dc`. 1250px horizon / 960px editorial rail.

**Technical philosophy:** Open source HTML/CSS/JS. Platform independence. No framework lock-in. PHP for form handling only. GitHub for version control. BBEdit (primary editor since 1994). Photoshop (since 1989). Obsidian for knowledge management. Scrivener for manuscript. AI tools: Claude (primary), Gemini (code assistance), NotebookLM (episode structure), ElevenLabs (sound design).

**Homepage structure (thinkamigo.com):**
- Hero — current Substack post, rotates with the practice
- Row one — Consulting, Heritage, Storytelling
- Row two — People, Expert for Hire, Media
- Award and partner strip — UK Gov, HMRC, Guardian, UNESCO, Arts Council, NTU

**Page types:**
- Profile pages (`amigo-firstname-lastname.html`) — collaborator pages use `people-intro-grid` with `biog-portrait`; founder page (amigo-paul-fillingham.html) uses `story-inline-image` within `text-rail`
- Service pages — consulting.html pattern with panoramic hero
- Proposition pages — expert-for-hire.html pattern, embedded form posting to dedicated PHP handler (`expert-engine.php`)
- OG images — 1200×630px JPEG, saved to `assets/img/og/PAGE-SLUG-og.jpg`. Substack post cover images 1080×1350px portrait format. Master Photoshop template with layer groups per page.
